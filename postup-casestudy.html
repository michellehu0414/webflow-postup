The challenge

PostUp is a new startup that allow freelancers and remote workers share tips, resources, and advice. Witnessing an uptick in feedback and discussions between users about finding good public places to work from, PostUp wants to create a better solution. create a subscription-based mobile app that addresses this trend for $5.99 a month. PostUp is a new startup that wants to make it easier for freelancers and remote workers to find great coffee shops and public spaces to do work from.

With a modified Google Ventures (GV) design sprint, I aimed to design the mobile app for PostUp over a course of five days that helps remote workers quickly find suitable public workspaces, helping PostUp enhance its user experience while opening a new revenue stream.

How can I connect remote workers with suitable public workspaces and help PostUp meet its business goals?

Although I obtained user research data from PostUp, it was limited to findings from 9 interviews, comprising only 8 quotes, a single user persona, and an audio and screen recording from one interview illustrating a participant guiding the Lead Researcher through her current solutions.

The solution

The PostUp mobile-app allows remote workers to locate nearby public spaces. Users may search locations by remote task, such as quiet work, meetings, and calls. Each public space has a comprehensive profile that displays details on essential work environments prioritized by remote workers, such as WiFi speed, restroom availability, noise-levels, table sizes, and more. Users have exclusive access reserve seating at partner locations that reserve seating for PostUp members and can see real-time updates on availability seating. All workspace details are generated by reviews posted by members.

Designing an MVP in 5 days

My design sprint adopts a modified version of Google Venture's (GV) design sprint.

This is a structured five-day process that rapidly solves critical business problems through design, prototyping, and testing ideas with users. Typically, a full-scale process involves a diverse team collaborating intensively in a series of exercises, such as mapping, sketching, and prototyping, to validate solutions quickly.

This project employs an adaptation that enables a solo designer to effectively carry out the streamlined process. Working independently, this process helped me quickly deliver a design and business solution to PostUp.

storyboarding pt.2
Completing the journey with user stories

This storyboard follows Nina's journey using PostUp in a new city. This was my first time designing a storyboard for UX design, as I've only developed storyboards for filmmaking! Using my past storyboarding experience for short films, I used a comic-style approach, which was incredibly helpful.

Illustrating Nina's journey with this technique allowed me to better immerse myself in her perspective and gain a better understanding of her needs and emotions. Continuously asking questions like "How might Nina react here?" and "What actions might Nina take next?" helped me to identify important touchpoints. By the end of the process, I was able to develop and refine both user stories and flows in one sitting.

This was a great experience! The visual aspect really deepened my empathy for users, and it made it much more efficient and enjoyable.

I intentionally placed Nina in an unfamiliar city to help me empathize with diverse user behaviors and possibilities.

With limited access to resources while traveling or on the go, remote workers in a new city potentially have greater needs compared to someone working in a familiar city. Therefore, I aimed to meet these needs to develop an adaptable and inclusive solution that accommodates diverse scenarios

On day 3, I expanded the 3-panel storyboard into a comprehensive comic-style narrative illustrating the entire remote work journey.

During my storyboard session, my user flow evolved. Below are some highlights:

What might Nina want to see when she opens PostUp?

Being in a new city, Nina likely has little knowledge of her surroundings, so she's probably keen to explore nearby workspaces. Therefore, the ideal home screen should show Nina a map that visually displays available options around her current location.

Where should Nina "search by task"?

Upon expanding my storyboard, I realized two design errors from days 1 and 2.

Day 1: Optimizing search process by taking users directly to search results after task-selection might backfire if the presets are even slight inaccurate.

Day 2: The lack of transparency behind the presets if located on the 'Discover' page might be confusing.

Solution: Create a 'Search page' that allows people to conduct a 'New search' or select 'Customizable presets'.

What if Nina realizes she has additional needs?

With a new 'Search' page, I was able to display the details on the preset filters to ensure transparency. As for my conflict regarding whether or not Nina should be able to adjust filters immediately after task selection, after reviewing my user research, I realized most people share the same preferences across all tasks! Being taken to a filters page each time Nina selects a preset could be counterproductive. She can also just conduct a new search. Besides, testing may reveal the ideal flow!

In addition to the workspace environment, what else might Nina want to learn?

The final highlight of my storyboard evolution was adding a 'Reviews' section for each workspace. After Nina learns about the workspace's details, she might want to see what other people have to say or seek more details, especially since that's what she's used to doing with her existing tools. For a more intuitive experience, users may navigate between the pages by swiping horizontally.

Additional Revisions

Changes since the storyboard:

Prioritizing convenience and accessibility of workspace details

My initial design divided the listing page into two sections: 'Details' and 'Reviews', navigable via a pagination bar for an intuitive experience. However, while prototyping I realized the pagination bar would sacrifice space for work environment details above the fold. I revisited user research and found remote workers mainly explore reviews in existing tools for workspace details due to mistrust of unreliable info. Recognizing the importance of accurate and accessible data, I adopted a single-page design that synthesizes workspace details submitted by other PostUp members.

Stashed Features

Due to time constraints, I couldn't design all potentially helpful features. With MVP design, I prioritized on solutions supported by strong evidence, excluding features with limited data. I felt comfortable doing this because I've learned that users often notice "missing" features and overlook unnecessary additions. Therefore, I planned to evaluate the need for the following features during testing and follow-up discussions:

Seating availability

Real-time seating availability was mentioned in user research, but only by one interviewee, lacking substantial data.

Reservation system

While seating reservation was not explicitly mentioned during user interviews, I speculated it would be helpful. However, due to the lack of seating reservation services across most coffee shops and libraries, I held off on implementing this feature.

Search bar in 'Discover' page

I didn't have enough time to prototype this alternative route, so I decided to prototype only one primary route and rely on testing to reveal alternative routes. My prototyped route at this point was navigating to the 'Search' page. However, I speculated that its location outside of home page ('Discover') compromised its visibility, thus accessibiilty. Scrolling through the map and searching via the search bar is the primary method across competitive products and is very straightforward, so I wanted to prioritze this route for testing.

Popular times

Displaying popular time was another solution I brainstormed. Similar to other pending features, it was not prototyped because no interviewees expressed a desire for it. However, IÂ speculated the need due to its presence in competitor products such as Google Maps, which I thought could be a user expectation. I speculated this would help further streamline the user flow.

Results

Overall, the feedback I received was overwhelmingly positive. Participants praised the user-friendly navigation, quick access to critical information, and visual design. They didn't find any critical information missing, but some suggested including additional helpful details that they don't find in their current methods, such as lighting and restroom capacity. Everyone completed their task very quickly and successfully.

The only errors occurred were some confusion with the copy and icons, which is reasonable given how much of the information included in this prototype are not typically found in other apps.

Business and Monetization Strategy

Based on the usability testing results, I brainstormed potential strategies aligned with the startup's requirement for a $5.99 monthly user subscription fee to increase business revenue streams. My focus involves brainstorming new features that drive spaner engagement with businesses featured on PostUp, thus amplifying revenue generation. Considering the growing trend toward remote work, I see a promising opportunity for a mutually beneficial collaboration between PostUp and local businesses. This collaboration could incentivize businesses to enhance their amenities, catering to remote workers and in turn enriching the overall user experience for Postup subscribers. Therefore, I propose an additional subscription and referral model that invites businesses to become a â€œPostUp Partnerâ€ to access premium in-app business features below:

Exclusive promotions

Partners can offer in-app promotions exclusive to PostUp users, encouraging user engagement and customer acquisition. Users may filter their search results for locations with ongoing promotions.

Reservation System

PostUp Partners can access the app's table reservation feature, enhancing user convenience and potentially attracting more customers. Users may filter their search results for locations that offer reservations.

Rewards System

The app incorporates two reward avenues: points and stamps. Users earn points via in-person purchases using a unique QR code and collect stamps by visiting partner locations. These accumulated points and stamps can be redeemed for rewards sponsored by PostUp. Users may filter search results for official partner locations to benefit from the rewards system.

Data Analytics

PostUp can offer valuable insights derived from user behavior, like traffic trends and user preferences, enabling partners to better understand their customer base. Partners may subscribe to access this data, enhancing their in-store strategies and improving user satisfaction.
