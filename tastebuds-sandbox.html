Guerilla testing

I prototoyped my sketches and conducted guerilla tests with 5 participants.

Outcomes

Users greatly enjoyed the inclusivity and diversity across the app (i.e. filters, food preferences, cuisine options).

They found the designs intuitive and straightforward.

Everyone successfully comprehended the intended purpose and functionalities.

Users independently employed different routes to explore dining options outside of the intended flow by using unique features.

Diners felt excited about the unique design solutions.

Most diners did not struggle with the design to rate stars.

Diners felt very pleased with the visual designs, such as graphics, colors, and more.

Merging the restaurant listing's home page and info page caused information overload to several diners.

Most diners were confused about some part of the design of reviews in the social feed.

Prototype errors frustrated some users.

Many did not detect the pagination bar in the restaurant listing.

The information architecture and visual hierarchy of the restaurant listing confused some users.

Diners use the map for a location based search.

Diners highly value deals and promotions.

The map was not visible to some diners.

Most diners did not use the slider to rate stars.

Recommendations

Given my findings, here are a few improvements and changes I planned to integrate into my low-fidelity wireframes:

Route 1

Prevent selection of contradicting restaurant qualities of strengths and weaknesses: For example, if user selects “authentic” as a strength, they cannot select “inauthentic”.

Enable users to tap or drag across the stars to rate.

Add a visual indication of the star-rating to show diners that they may rate from 0 and in increments of 0.5, accommodating the current mental model.

Label each increment of 0.5 to the rating system: This will standardize user’s rating scale to increase users’ trust in interpreting their friends’ reviews.

Route 2

Implement a carousel view of search results to enable users to compare restaurants more efficiently, removing the need to navigate in and out of restaurant listings to learn more.

Add a "map" button to the bottom of the list and carousel views of search results: This will allow users to easily pull up the map without reaching to drag the list down to view.

Add “sort by distance” into search filters.

Incorporate “deals” into the list view of search results: Since each participant tapped on the “deal” feature first upon visiting the restaurant listing page, this may be an important feature for them to see first to expedite decision-making. I may also incorporate this into the filters.
