Tools

PostUp is a new startup that allow freelancers and remote workers share tips and advice. Witnessing an uptick in feedback and discussions on how to find good public places to work from, PostUp wants to leverage this opportunity to create a subscription-based mobile app that addresses this trend for $5.99 a month.

With a modified Google Ventures (GV) design sprint, I aimed to design the mobile app for PostUp over a course of five days that helps remote workers quickly find suitable public workspaces, helping PostUp enhance its user experience while opening a new revenue stream.

How might we connect remote workers with suitable public workspaces, while meeting PostUp's business goals?

research process

Designing an MVP prototype in 5 days

My design sprint adopts a modified Google Venture's (GV) design sprint.

This is a structured five-day process aimed at rapidly solving critical business problems through design, prototyping, and testing ideas with users. Typically, a full-scale process involves a diverse team collaborating intensively in a series of exercises, such as mapping, sketching, and prototyping, to validate solutions quickly. This project employs an adaptation that enables a solo designer to effectively carry out the streamlined process. Working independently, this process helped me quickly deliver a design and business solution to PostUp.

While developing this panel, I identified two key design errors from day 1 and day 2. Initially, on day 1, I presumed some users may need to adjust filters after selecting their task for a more customized experience.

"Sometimes, removing just one button can significantly enhance the user experience." â€” Day 1

Additionally, on day 2, the first panel in my 3-panel storyboard depicted a filter page where users input their tasks at the top but failed to show the corresponding filters, potentially causing confusion.

However, upon reviewing my user research, I realized that most remote workers' share the same work environment preferences, as remote tasks are not particularly complex. Therefore, the filters I design for each task are likely to align with most users' preferences.

To address these challenges, I implemented a revised search approach for Annie by creating a separate search page that provides two distinct methods:

  • Annie can begin a new customized search by setting personalized filters according to her specific requirements.
  • Alternatively, Annie can opt for a quick search feature, pressing a button that instantly identifies suitable locations aligned with their task of the day. Remote workers can further adjust these results for a more tailored experience.

This redesign aims to accommodate diverse needs and preferences among remote workers, offering greater transparency in the search process. Ultimately, it fosters a more personalized and comprehensive user experience that caters to varying preferences and tasks.

Solution

Search Results

Displaying essential information as soon as possible to help remote workers expedite decision-making.

The search results are designed to present essential information that remote workers prioritize in their decision-making and optimize their decision process. It includes not only the distance but also the time it takes to walk to the location. It directly indicates the type of work that the place is suitable for, which includes quiet work, meetings, and calls. It also displays the variety of amenities offered and whether or not there is seating available, which is only displayed if the establishment is a participating PostUp partner.

walk time on search results

User research revealed that many remote workers visit urban cities. Therefore, I decided to display the walk time instead of commute time with a car. To make this experience intuitive, the search results would display commute time by car if the user is in a suburban area with poor walkability. The engineering necessary for this function would simply rely on Google Maps information. However, I planned to create a search filter for remote workers to select the type of commute they want to see.

Listing Page

Each listing page displays more in-depth information on the work environment with a strong above-the-fold design. I ensured all of the most pertinent information are visible without scrolling, so remote workers can gain a comprehensive understanding of the workspace efficiently at first glance. Instead of photos of food, the listing focuses on photos of the interior.

work environment details

To minimize clutter to prevent information overload, IÂ opted for minimal copy to convey the work environment details. To address any confusion, this page aims to elaborate on the details, inspired by clothing size charts.

Storyboard Revisions

My prototype made a few changes to the designs depicted in my storyboard:

streamlining the listing page

Previously, my storyboard segmented the listing page into two sectionsâ€”details and reviews, which users can seamlessly navigate with a pagination bar. This design aimed to facilitate an intuitive and organized experience for remote workers to navigate between the pages. However, when I was designing the screen, I found that implementing the pagination bar would sacrifice space for work environment details above the fold.

To determine the more effective design approach, I revisited my research for more insights into user behaviors. I discovered that remote workers (1) primarily explore reviews to learn about workspace amenities and (2) rely on reviews due to mistrust shaped by past experiences with inaccurate or unavailable information. This reinforced my understanding on the criticality of accuracy and accessibility of essential information. To draw more inspiration, IÂ alsoÂ revisited my lightning demos of Airbnb's listing page, which adopts a single-page design that places guest reviews in the middle.

With these new insights, I reassessed my design priorities and speculated that accurate information on the work environment might reduce the need for remote workers to evaluate the reviews. Therefore, I decided to forfeit the pagination bar and opt for a single-page design.

Pending Features

Given the time constraints, I didn't have time to design the few features that could potentially be helpful. Moreover, IÂ wanted to avoid unnecessary features to adhere to MVPÂ design principles, so I decided to prioritize designs with overwhelming evidence and exclude any functionalities with low data points. In my experience, users are more likely to notice and express what's missing, and less likely to validate unnecessary features unless they contribute to negative usability experiences such as information overload. Therefore, I planned to rely on usability testing to assess the necessity for the following features:

displaying seating availability

The participant interviewed during user research mentioned that information on seating availability would aid her search process. However, this was not presented in the user persona and the interviewed participant was the only user who expressed this desire.

implementing a reservation system

While a need for a reservation tool was not explicitly stated in user research, I speculated it would be helpful to address the similar need for displaying seating availability. Moreover, my exclusion is due to the lack of reservations for spaces like coffee shops and libraries.

prototyping the search bar on the discover page

Usually, I prototype various routes for users to complete their user journey. However, given the time constraints, I decided to prototype only one route and rely on testing to reveal the primary and alternative routes. I speculated that the alternative primary route would search via the search bar placed at the top of the Discover page. My prototype at this point only designed the route for participants to search for options via "search by task" located only on the Search page. While IÂ designed this route to optimize the user flow, I speculated that its location outside of the Discover page compromised its accessibility, making it less likely the primary route.

displaying popular times

While the need for information on the trend in popular times of a workspace was not explicitly expressed in user research, IÂ speculated the need due to its presence in competitive products. I anticipated it being a user expectation shaped by competitor products like Google Maps. Moreover, given remote workers' preference for less crowded spaces, I believed in its potential to cater to enhance the user flow.

Business and Monetization Strategy

Based on the usability testing results, I brainstormed potential strategies aligned with the startup's requirement for a $5.99 monthly user subscription fee to increase business revenue streams. My focus involves brainstorming new features that drive stronger engagement with businesses featured on PostUp, thus amplifying revenue generation. Considering the growing trend toward remote work, I see a promising opportunity for a mutually beneficial collaboration between PostUp and local businesses. This collaboration could incentivize businesses to enhance their amenities, catering to remote workers and in turn enriching the overall user experience for Postup subscribers. Therefore, I propose an additional subscription and referral model that invites businesses to become a â€œPostUp Partnerâ€ to access premium in-app business features below:

exclusive promotions

Partners can offer in-app promotions exclusive to PostUp users, encouraging user engagement and customer acquisition. Users may filter their search results for locations with ongoing promotions.

Reservation System

PostUp Partners can access the app's table reservation feature, enhancing user convenience and potentially attracting more customers. Users may filter their search results for locations that offer reservations.

Rewards System

The app incorporates two reward avenues: points and stamps. Users earn points via in-person purchases using a unique QR code and collect stamps by visiting partner locations. These accumulated points and stamps can be redeemed for rewards sponsored by PostUp. Users may filter search results for official partner locations to benefit from the rewards system.

Data Analytics

PostUp can offer valuable insights derived from user behavior, like traffic trends and user preferences, enabling partners to better understand their customer base. Partners may subscribe to access this data, enhancing their in-store strategies and improving user satisfaction.

Before IÂ forget, absolutely NO sponsored content

Over-commercialization, as observed in platforms like Yelp, Instagram, TikTok, and others, frequently disrupts the core user experience. When profit-driven motives overshadow the genuine needs of users, users experience cluttered interfaces, intrusive advertisements, or a departure from what truly matters to them. Therefore, my proposed design solutions intentionally exclude sponsored content. Instead, they prioritize user-centric monetization strategies to generate revenue without compromising user experience. By focusing on this balance, the aim is to maintain a positive user journey and avoid overwhelming users with commercial intent, ensuring sustained engagement and satisfaction.

Overall, my strategies aim to increase client acquisition, boost conversion, drive app engagement, attract business partnerships, and establish a sustainable revenue model while enhancing the overall remote working experience throughout their user journey. In sum, these strategies offer additional revenue streams for PostUp while boosting the app's value proposition.
